Phase I: Synthesis - Beep Boop 🤖

After my second season of FIRST Robotics, I was stuck in a creative rut—bored with the same design work and frustrated by endless internal politics. I needed a new challenge to push me in ways I hadn’t been before.

That’s when I conversed with my mentor, Ming, who was part of the larger SAS Robotics program. We talked about potential summer projects, and when the MATE ROV competition came up, I instantly knew this was it. I wanted to build something that would push us beyond what we thought possible.

💡
A little about the MATE ROV competition: it's a global challenge in which teams build remote-operated underwater vehicles to tackle simulated marine tasks, such as repairing cables, collecting data, and testing their engineering and problem-solving skills.

Two months later, I had a team ready to take on the challenge. With our FRC experience, I thought it would be smooth sailing. I was wrong. This wouldn’t be easy—it would test everything we knew.

And so began the journey of RED V—a journey that would stretch our limits, push our capabilities, and teach us more than we ever expected.

Phase II: Think! Think! Think!

Story

Procrastination hit us hard. What was supposed to be a well-paced design process turned into a mad dash as deadlines loomed. With time running out, I locked myself in my room, determined to bring order to the chaos. I dove into the MATE ROV rulebook, searching for a way to simplify our approach. After days of intense focus, I distilled our strategy into three guiding principles: modularity, adaptability, and simplicity.

3 Principles- Why?

  1. Modularity meant every part of RED V could be swapped or adjusted with minimal effort. We needed the flexibility to fix issues quickly, knowing things would inevitably go wrong.
  2. Adaptability ensured our ROV could handle various tasks without requiring major changes, making it versatile enough for the unpredictable underwater environment.
  3. Finally, simplicity was crucial—our design had to be straightforward and functional, avoiding unnecessary complexity that could trip us up.

With these principles in mind, I spent a week CAD designing the entire robot. It was a race against time, balancing the need for precision with the urgency of our deadline. My team, eager but inexperienced, took on the basic tasks, allowing me to focus on the critical decisions. Their support kept us moving, even as the pressure mounted.

Despite the challenges, we emerged with a solid plan. The design was complete, and we were ready to move on to the next phase: building RED V. The most challenging part was still ahead, but we had a clear direction—and that was enough to keep us going.

2.1 Robot Specifications

RED V is a compact ROV, measuring 47.5 cm x 34 cm x 32.5 cm and weighing 6.2 kg with the tether. Its sturdy aluminium frame makes it easy to adjust and repair. With six thrusters, RED V moves smoothly and precisely. The electronics are protected in a waterproof box, ensuring everything works underwater. I Designed it to be simple and adaptable; RED V is ready to destroy the competition (or so I thought).

2.2 Frame

Using the fewest thrusters possible, I aimed to achieve superior thrust vector control with six degrees of freedom– essentially giving the ROV the ability to move its "hand" (the manipulator) freely in any direction for complex tasks. To achieve this, I explored complex shapes like octagons and dodecahedrons, but these proved too complicated to build and program. After multiple iterations, I found that a cuboid was the most straightforward and space-efficient design.

The decision to use 20x20 mm T-slot aluminium rails, inspired by my 3D printer, provided the perfect balance of lightness and strength. This modular design allowed us to easily swap out components, adjust positions, and optimize the robot as needed.

This choice paid off when I used robust, cost-effective 90-degree steel plates, enabling us to assemble the frame in under 30 minutes. The modular setup also made it easy to add 3D-printed legs to reduce stress on the manipulator and bottom camera and adjust buoyancy late in the season.

2.3 Manipulator

Early on, I recognized that a reliable manipulator was crucial, as it’s the only component directly interacting with the props. Due to limited time and the challenges of prototyping a new claw design, I drew inspiration from the well-established Blue Robotics subsea gripper. However, I made key adaptations, incorporating 3D-printed parts and aluminium CNC plates secured with screws and threaded inserts to enhance durability and simplify repairs.

While I initially considered using a lead screw design similar to Blue Robotics, I ultimately chose a slider-crank mechanism powered by an affordable waterproof servo (LW-25MG) for its ease of waterproofing and assembly. To add versatility, I integrated a second LW-25MG servo with a gear system to rotate the gripper. This allowed the claw to adjust angles for different tasks—vertical for inter-ray cable work and horizontal for seagrass pruning. To improve grip, I lined the manipulator’s edges with rubber tape.

The LW-25MG servo, with its high torque of 25 kg and built-in waterproofing, was ideal for quickly and reliably operating the claw. This setup ensured the manipulator could handle various tasks without losing grip. Ultimately, I built a functional and cost-effective claw for $143.68, which offers more functionality at a fraction of the cost of the Newton Subsea Gripper ($590).

2.4 Buoyancy System

Achieving neutral buoyancy was crucial for RED V to maintain stable and precise control underwater, allowing the robot to remain in place without constantly fighting gravity or buoyancy. The buoyancy system was designed using PVC pipes with a 63 mm outer diameter, chosen for their consistent buoyant force across varying depths and ease of mounting.

Calculating Gravitational Force

The gravitational force acting on the ROV %%( F_g )%% is calculated as:

$$F_g = m \times g$$

Where:

  • %%m%% is the mass of the ROV, which is %%5.2 kg%%,
  • %%g%% is the acceleration due to gravity %%( 9.81\text{m/s}^2 )%%.

So,

$$F_g = 5.2\text{kg} \times 9.81\text{m/s}^2 = 51.012\text{N}$$

This gravitational force pulls the ROV downward.

Buoyant Force

The buoyant force %%( F_b )%% acting on the ROV, which counteracts the gravitational force, is determined using the equation:

$$F_b = \rho \times V \times g$$

Where:

  • %%\rho%% is the density of water %%( 1000\text{kg/m}^3 )%%,
  • %%V%% is the volume of air in the PVC pipes,
  • %%g%% is the acceleration due to gravity %%( 9.81\text{m/s}^2 )%%.

The volume %%( V )%% of air in the PVC pipes is calculated by:

$$V = \pi \times (r^2) \times L$$

Where:

  • %%r%% is the inner radius of the PVC pipe, %%( 30 \times 10^{-3}\text{m} )%%,
  • %%L%% is the length of the pipe.

Substituting the values:

$$F_b = 1000 \times \left(\pi \times \left(30 \times 10^{-3}\right)^2 \times 0.92\right) \times 9.81 = 8.12\pi \times 9.81$$

$$\approx 25.5\text{N}$$

This buoyant force acts upward, counteracting the gravitational force.

Adjusting Buoyancy

Initially, the buoyant force was insufficient to fully counteract the gravitational force, leading to the need to fine-tune the PVC pipe length. After testing, the required length of PVC was adjusted to 22 cm to achieve neutral buoyancy.

Adjusting Buoyancy Calculation

However, during real-life testing, additional weights were added to the ROV. To maintain neutral buoyancy, I calculated the length of PVC to remove:

$$\text{Length Of PVC to Remove} = \frac{\text{Mass of Weights Added to the ROV}}{\rho \times \text{Area of PVC}}$$

Where:

  • The mass of the weights added was %%1.94 kg%%,
  • The area of the PVC pipe cross-section is calculated as %%\pi \times (30 \times 10^{-3})^2%%.

Substituting the values:

$$\text{Length Of PVC to Remove} = \frac{1.94}{1000 \times \pi \times \left(30 \times 10^{-3}\right)^2} \approx 70\text{cm}$$

This adjustment ensured that the ROV remained neutrally buoyant.

Practical Adjustments

To counterbalance the added weight of the manipulator at the front of the ROV, smaller PVC pipes with a 23 mm outer diameter were added. This prevented the ROV from tilting forward, keeping it level and stable during operation.

The PVC pipes were mounted using custom CNC plates and locally purchased angular gussets, allowing for easy adjustments and repositioning as needed. This setup also allowed the buoyancy tubes to pivot out of the way for quick access to the electronics and repairs.

By carefully calculating and adjusting the buoyancy system, RED V maintained neutral buoyancy, enhancing its manoeuvrability and stability in the water.

2.5 Propulsion

RED V is powered by six T-60 thrusters strategically arranged to provide optimal thrust vector control with six degrees of freedom. The propulsion system was carefully designed to ensure precise and intuitive manoeuvrability.

Thruster Arrangement

The propulsion system features four T-60 thrusters positioned at 45-degree angles within the cuboidal frame, forming an X-shaped configuration. This setup allows RED V to move forward and backwards and translate left and right efficiently. Additionally, parallel to the ROV, two thrusters are mounted on opposite sides of the frame to control vertical movement and provide fine adjustments to pitch and roll.

This X-shaped configuration was selected for its ability to maximize degrees of freedom while minimizing the number of thrusters. This balances power requirements and keeps the system cost-effective. The load is evenly distributed among the four primary thrusters, ensuring stability and smooth control during complex manoeuvres.

🚨
Pitch Control and Software Integration
While the X-shaped thruster configuration provides excellent control, it does have limited pitch control. To address this, a gyroscope was integrated into the ROV's software, allowing for fine-tuned adjustments to maintain balance. Combined with the carefully calibrated buoyancy system, this setup ensures that RED V remains stable and precise in all its movements.

Thrust Capabilities

Each T-60 thruster generates 1.65 kg of thrust, sufficient to meet the competition's requirements, including lifting a maximum load of 1 kg. The choice of T-60 thrusters ($50) over the more expensive Blue Robotics T200 thrusters ($200) was made to maintain cost-effectiveness without compromising performance. The T-60 thrusters are also more compact, making them an ideal fit for RED V's design.

Mounting and Modularity

The thrusters are mounted using 3D-printed brackets that slide into the T-slot aluminium frame. This modular design allows for quick and easy replacements or adjustments, ensuring issues can be resolved swiftly with minimal downtime. Replacing a thruster in minutes is critical in maintaining operational readiness during competition.

Phase III: Tangle Me to Death...

Story

The initial design phase felt like a breeze. Every detail was meticulously planned, down to the last screw, and we felt unstoppable. Supply issues? Minor hiccups. We were so confident that we even joked about finishing within a month—oh, how naive we were.

Then came the electronics phase. It seemed simple enough: connect some wires, do a bit of soldering, and voilà, we’re done. Or so we thought. Little did we know that electronics—especially waterproofing—would become our worst nightmare.

Water, that seemingly harmless liquid, became our biggest enemy. The number of components we fried due to the tiniest leaks was staggering. Every mistake, no matter how small, sets us back significantly.

Our problems all boiled down to a few seemingly minor design flaws. First, our initial design had a horizontal plate blocking components' access, making repairs a nightmare. Then, there was a tiny protrusion beyond the box’s limit—barely noticeable, but it let water seep in like a flood.

Our solution? We went vertical. We redesigned the layout to ensure nothing protruded beyond the waterproof box. It was a hard lesson but a turning point.

Looking back, this period—what I now call "Integration Hell Love ❤️" —was both the most challenging and rewarding. It was during this time that my team truly bonded. We laughed, we struggled, and we came out stronger for it. In the end, that made all the difference.

3.1 Cameras

RED V is equipped with two Sony IMX323 HD (1080P) USB cameras. The front camera overlooks the manipulator for precise task control, while the bottom camera provides a comprehensive view of the seafloor for detailed operations like creating mosaics. The cameras are housed in waterproof GoPro cases, reinforced with resin, and connected to the waterproof Bopla Bocube via wet-link penetrators. These feeds are directly linked to the Raspberry Pi, ensuring real-time monitoring and control for the pilot.

3.2 Depth Sensor

The depth sensor is essential for maintaining the ROV's altitude underwater, aiding in precise task execution. Mounted securely and calibrated for accuracy, the sensor feeds real-time depth data into the control system via the Raspberry Pi. This allows the pilot to keep the ROV stable and at the correct depth, enhancing control and mission efficiency.

3.3 On-Board Electronics

RED V’s onboard electronics system has undergone two major iterations to address several critical issues that arose during the initial design. The first version of the system was plagued with problems such as poor soldering, suboptimal cable management, and the use of spade connectors, all of which contributed to frequent leaks and made the system nearly impossible to repair efficiently. This version also used horizontal CNC plates that further complicated access to the electronics, making repairs a daunting task.

To resolve these issues, the second iteration introduced a much-improved design. The electronics are now housed in a larger, more spacious Bopla Bocube (210 x 191 mm x 90 mm), a weatherproof box that offers better protection and easier access. One of the key innovations in this redesign was the introduction of 3D-printed vertical plates, which replaced the horizontal configuration. These vertical plates allow the electronics to be easily slid in and out of the racks, significantly simplifying the process of making repairs or modifications.

The modularity of this new setup was enhanced by using bullet connectors and DuPont cables, allowing components to be connected and disconnected easily. This change made the system more reliable and dramatically reduced the build time—from over a month in the first iteration to just three days in the second.

The core components of the onboard electronics system include six EMAX Bullet ESCs, a Pixhawk 2.4.8 autopilot, a Raspberry Pi 4B, power distribution terminals, two buck converters, depth sensors, and two HD cameras. The system’s waterproofing is ensured by using wet-link penetrators, which, despite their higher cost, were chosen for their reusability and reliability, making future repairs and modifications straightforward.

4.1 Basic Control Software

Our robot’s key functions, like movement and manipulator control, are managed by the Ardusub system from Blue Robotics. This setup includes a Raspberry Pi as the companion computer, a Pixhawk 2.4.8 as the autopilot, and a topside control station.

Since ArduSub uses the MAVLink protocol, we employed the Pymavlink library with the mavutil module in Python to handle communications. We created movement functions and consolidated them into a single file for easy import, reducing redundancy and simplifying debugging.

We chose Ardusub for its reliability and strong community support, which minimized issues and kept the project on schedule.
